There is no FTL travel; no wormholes, no teleportation, no "warp-drive."
However, relativistic speeds still cause significant time dilation, and are achievable through energy and procedural breakthroughs. This process means it is "trivial" to send cargo, and living things, large distances in a locally short amount of time. You could jump on a ship, and travel to the nearest star in a little over a year using Earth's gravity acceleration.

# Procedure
1. Construct a ship which consists majority of cargo.
2. Construct an "accelerator" on the host planet.
3. **MAGIC:** Focus a beam onto the ship which accelerates the ship at the desired gravity acceleration.
4. When the ship is half-way to the destination, host planet stops acceleration
5. Ship pivots 180 degrees.
6. **MAGIC:** convert kinetic energy into work to power the ship systems, radiate excess energy as heat. Produce chemical energy for landing.
7. Land at destination, consume chemical energy made during deceleration.

There are a few "holes" in the procedure. The biggest one is probably how the ship manages to self-decelerate while recovering some of the energy as work. The second is how to continuously accelerate the ship from a planet for years, and at least half the distance to the target. Long trips might be impossible, and more energy needs to be sent due to the ship moving away, creating a sort of Doppler-effect at the receiving end. This might be negated at a point by the local time dilation, where the occupants actually need less energy for acceleration because less time is passing.

# Some Numbers

If accelerating at 1.02Gs (about 10m/s^2), and using half the distance for deceleration, doubling the local travel time quadruples the distance traveled.

| Travel Time (Local) | Distance      |
| ------------------- | ------------- |
| 3 months            | 1.87 L-months |
| 6 months            | 7.5 L-months  |
| 1 year              | 2.5 L-years   |
| 2 years             | 10 L-years    |
| 4 years             | 40 L-years    |
The travel time for the ship must reach 1 month and 6 days before the benefits of time dilation appear. To be more exact, 0.1 years is the point at which the rate of distance traveled outpaces the rate of local time passed for the ship.

To observers on Earth, the ship travels at least as long as the distance of the journey. As I understand it, the time on Earth is effectively equal to the distance the ship travels. To reach the nearest star, Proxima Centauri at ~4.25 L-years, the travelers would have to wait ~1.304 years, or about 15 months and 20 days.

# Time Perception Tricks
1. Using anesthetics to help passengers remain unconscious for longer.
2. Cooling bodies + drugs to slow metabolism of passengers to reduce aging during the journey, and reduce the disconnect between time felt by the mind and time felt by the body.
3. Light sources dimming to simulate shorter days and longer nights.

To be optimistic, let's say travelers consciously experience 20% of the travel time. Biologically, 40% of the travel time is experienced. For a 6 month experience, the ship travels for 2.5 years, about 15 L-years are covered, and the traveler ages 1 year.

When accounting for time passage techniques, the journey to Proxima Centauri is perceived to be about 3 months and 4 days, and biologically is under 8 months.

| Perceived | Biological | Ship       | Distance      |
| --------- | ---------- | ---------- | ------------- |
| 3 months  | 6 months   | 1.25 years | ~3.9 L-years  |
| 6 months  | 12 months  | 2.5 years  | ~15.6 L-years |
| 9 months  | 18 months  | 3.75 years | ~35.2 L-years |
| 12 months | 24 months  | 5 years    | 62.5 L-years  |
There is a dramatic consequence for travelers. The difference between perceived time and biological time can be literally equated to shortening someone's life. Taking a one year journey effectively removes a year of life, and depending on what the individual was doing over that time, can be considered a loss of two years.

This problem can be catastrophic for the development of children, their body seeming to mature at twice the normal rate, losing valuable time. Most people don't dare traveling more than 30 L-years in their entire lives. Sending a newborn on a ten year journey effectively places them into the body of a twenty-year-old.

# Purpose

Due to the distances, and time, between stars and planets, travel is almost entirely a money sink. Few organizations actually benefit from sending groups of people and equipment on one-way trips. Therefore, any journeys made are for the long-term benefit to humanity. Simply lessening the chances of going extinct is enough for some to send people tens or hundreds of light-years away.

Some organizations are entirely built on toiling for a decade or two, and stabilizing a planet for hundreds or thousands of years. For some of the richest alive, planting the seeds of a thousand year civilization named after them is enough.

Military organizations, or rather a large group of people who want to spend their lives defending others, tend to fare quite well. Well enough that some just continue to split and spread to other stars.

Space travel is not so different from tribal migration. A group hops to a new island, and their great grandchildren end up doing the same thing again some 50 years later.
